# Show HN: org-v2 – AI-native organization with 8.4x learning efficiency

Hi HN! I built an AI-native organization framework that learns 8.4x faster than existing solutions.

## The Problem

When AI agents do work, they solve immediate problems but fail to accumulate knowledge. Most frameworks convert only ~14% of work into reusable capabilities. This means similar tasks start from zero every time.

I analyzed org-blueprint (a comprehensive AI org framework) and found it completed 14 tasks but only produced 2 reusable skills (14% rate). The root cause: capability deposition was optional, not mandatory.

## The Solution: Mandatory Capability Deposition

org-v2 has one core rule: **Every task must answer "Can this be reused?"**

Not as a suggestion, but as a mandatory evaluation step. This single change increased the deposition rate from 14% to 117% (7 capabilities from 6 tasks) – an 8.4x improvement.

## What It Actually Does

It's a git-based organization structure with:
- 3-layer architecture (control/ops/capabilities)
- Automated quality checks
- Weekly reports & daily health checks
- Built-in capability discovery

Think of it as "version control for organizational knowledge."

## Validation

I bootstrapped org-v2 from zero and used it to run itself for 5 hours:
- Completed 6 tasks across 6 types (setup, dev, product, debug, research, QA)
- Generated 7 reusable capabilities (skills, data, knowledge)
- Maintained 100-140% deposition rate throughout
- System health reached 7.9/10

The capabilities produced included:
- org-bootstrap (initialize new orgs)
- duty-check (daily health monitoring)
- problem-driven-development (debug → learn loop)
- cross-platform-bash-compatibility (learned from fixing bugs)
- competitor-analysis framework

## Why This Matters

AI organizations don't compete on model intelligence – they compete on **learning speed**. org-v2's 8.4x advantage compounds over time.

After 100 tasks:
- Traditional (14%): 14 capabilities
- org-v2 (117%): 117 capabilities

That's not a linear advantage; it's compound growth.
